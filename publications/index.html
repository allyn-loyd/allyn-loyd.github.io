  1. Evolution of Wavelets and Hardware for Satellite Image Compression
    Allyn Loyd and Jason A. Yoder
    In Computational Intelligence, 2026
    Abs DOI

    When designing a system to transmit satellite images, it is important to balance the need for a high compression rate with the ability to accurately reconstruct them. The discrete wavelet transform (DWT) can be used to compress images and can be fine-tuned for specific classes of images, but it requires substantial hardware resources to perform floating-point multiplications. Separate research efforts to evolve wavelets and hardware multipliers have been performed, but not together. This work explores the use of several evolutionary and coevolutionary algorithms to address this gap, focusing on different ways of combining wavelets and hardware. The first algorithm evolves them in two separate stages, a second evolves them in pairs, and the third algorithm cooperatively coevolves two populations of wavelets and hardware. The results indicate that problem decomposition is most beneficial when evolution allows individual components to be optimized separately. Additionally, our evolved wavelets show improved accuracy over existing wavelets at high compression ratios, and the evolved hardware uses four times fewer components as a human-designed approach. This work demonstrates strong potential for effective image compression and, more broadly, the use of coevolutionary algorithms to fine-tune circuit design alongside secondary systems defining the needs of those circuits.

  1. Bitstream Evolution: An Open-Source FPGA Intrinsic Evolvable Hardware Toolkit
    Allyn Loyd, Daniel Gaull, Logan Manthey, and 3 more authors
    IEEE Access, 2025
    Abs DOI

    The field of intrinsic, bitstream-level, evolvable hardware has recently been resurrected and interest in the area has begun to grow. This paper presents a low barrier-to-entry software and hardware platform allowing evolvable hardware researchers to conduct experiments, visualize results, and share data. The system’s open-source software is designed to enable iterative enhancements so that contributors from around the world can participate. To encourage community development, we detail the process and results of several of our experiments, which can be used as a reference for further study. Additionally, we discuss the results of our experiments, focusing in particular on their implications with respect to the question of robustness and transferability of evolved hardware circuits. Our results demonstrate significant potential for evolving circuits that not only maintain long-term functionality but can also operate effectively outside their original evolutionary environments. These results demonstrate the feasibility of intrinsic, bitstream level evolution which, in the long run, could increase the efficiency and lower the power requirements of modern computing and artificial intelligence systems. We discuss open questions for the field and directions for future work including stability, transferability, robustness, quality diversity, signal processing, and security.

  1. Reversibility of Stranded Cellular Automata
    Allyn Loyd and Joshua Holden
    Mathematical Sciences Technical Reports (MSTR), 2023
    Abs HTML

    Cellular automata, such as the Stranded Cellular Automaton (SCA) model created by Joshua and Lana Holden, can be used to model weaving patterns. Similar models can be constructed to model macrame patterns, where strands are knotted together. If a rule is injective, then it is reversible. If a rule is surjective, then every configuration has at least one predecessor. In this paper, we will discuss the injectivity and surjectivity of several new SCA models in order to find reversible rules. We will also analyze the number of configurations with no predecessors and the number of configurations that map to the same successor.
